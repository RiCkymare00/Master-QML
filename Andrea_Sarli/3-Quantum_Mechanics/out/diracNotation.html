Introduction

In quantum mechanics, physical systems are described by state vectors in a complex Hilbert space. Dirac notation is a powerful formalism that provides a concise and abstract representation of these vectors.

From a geometric perspective, a ket ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ can be visualized as a vector in an abstract complex vector space. Just like arrows in Euclidean space represent direction and magnitude, quantum state vectors represent the "direction" of the system's state in Hilbert space. The length (or norm) of the ket corresponds to the probability amplitude, and only the relative directions (angles) between state vectors have physical meaning, as global phases do not affect measurable quantities.

A state vector is denoted by a ket:

∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle ∣ψ⟩

where ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ is an element of a Hilbert space H\mathcal{H}H, a complete complex vector space equipped with an inner product. The ket represents the abstract quantum state of a physical system.

In practice, ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ can be represented in different bases (such as position or momentum), and its components depend on the chosen basis. The ket notation encapsulates all measurable information about the quantum system and evolves according to the Schrödinger equation in the time-dependent case.

Kets are most commonly used to represent the quantum states of physical systems, such as the position, momentum, or spin state of a particle. For example, the ket ∣x⟩|x\rangle∣x⟩ denotes a state of definite position, while ∣p⟩|p\rangle∣p⟩ denotes a state of definite momentum. In addition to representing physical states, kets can also represent abstract vectors in Hilbert space that are not directly measurable, such as superpositions of basis states or unphysical trial states used in computations.

A ket can also represent a basis state, such as a state of definite position |x\rangle, definite momentum |p\rangle, or a discrete basis state like |i\rangle. These basis states form the building blocks used to expand more general quantum states through linear combinations.

1.1 Expansion of a Quantum State in a Basis

Any quantum state ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ can be written as a linear combination of basis vectors in a Hilbert space. If {∣i⟩}\{|i\rangle\}{∣i⟩} is an orthonormal basis, then:

∣ψ⟩=∑ici∣i⟩|\psi\rangle = \sum_i c_i |i\rangle ∣ψ⟩=i∑​ci​∣i⟩

where each coefficient cic_ici​ is a complex number given by the projection of the state onto the basis vector:

ci=⟨i∣ψ⟩c_i = \langle i|\psi\rangle ci​=⟨i∣ψ⟩

This decomposition expresses ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ as a superposition of the basis states. The set {∣i⟩}\{|i\rangle\}{∣i⟩} can represent position eigenstates, momentum eigenstates, energy eigenstates, or any other complete orthonormal basis appropriate to the quantum system being studied.

The expansion is central to the mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics because it allows us to work with arbitrary states using known basis functions and simplifies calculations involving operators and measurements.

3. Operators in Dirac Notation

In quantum mechanics, operators are fundamental mathematical objects that act on state vectors (kets) in a Hilbert space to represent physical observables such as position, momentum, energy, and angular momentum.

Given an operator A^\hat{A}A^ and a state vector ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩, the action of A^\hat{A}A^ on ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ is written as:

A^∣ψ⟩\hat{A}|\psi\rangle A^∣ψ⟩

This operation produces a new state vector in the same Hilbert space. Operators obey the rules of linear algebra and can be composed, added, and multiplied. They play a central role in the mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics.

3.1 Properties of Operators

Operators in quantum mechanics satisfy several important properties:

  • Linearity: An operator A^\hat{A}A^ is linear if it satisfies:

    A^(a∣ψ⟩+b∣ϕ⟩)=aA^∣ψ⟩+bA^∣ϕ⟩\hat{A}(a|\psi\rangle + b|\phi\rangle) = a\hat{A}|\psi\rangle + b\hat{A}|\phi\rangle A^(a∣ψ⟩+b∣ϕ⟩)=aA^∣ψ⟩+bA^∣ϕ⟩

    for all complex numbers a,ba, ba,b and vectors ∣ψ⟩,∣ϕ⟩|\psi\rangle, |\phi\rangle∣ψ⟩,∣ϕ⟩. All physical operators are assumed to be linear.

  • Commutativity and Non-Commutativity: In general, operators do not commute. The commutator of two operators A^\hat{A}A^ and B^\hat{B}B^ is defined as:

    [A^,B^]=A^B^−B^A^[\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = \hat{A}\hat{B} - \hat{B}\hat{A} [A^,B^]=A^B^−B^A^

    If [A^,B^]=0[\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = 0[A^,B^]=0, the operators A^\hat{A}A^ and B^\hat{B}B^ are said to commute. If the commutator is non-zero, they do not commute. Commutators are especially important for physical observables, like position and momentum.

  • Hermitian Operators: An operator A^\hat{A}A^ is Hermitian (or self-adjoint) if it satisfies:

    A^=A^†\hat{A} = \hat{A}^\dagger A^=A^†

    Hermitian operators have several key properties:

    • Their eigenvalues are always real, ensuring that measurements of physical observables yield real numbers.
    • Their eigenvectors form an orthonormal basis, making it possible to expand quantum states in terms of these eigenvectors.

  • Unitary Operators: An operator U^\hat{U}U^ is unitary if it satisfies:

    U^†U^=U^U^†=I\hat{U}^\dagger \hat{U} = \hat{U} \hat{U}^\dagger = I U^†U^=U^U^†=I

    where III is the identity operator. Unitary operators preserve the norm of vectors and are important in describing time evolution and symmetries in quantum mechanics.

3.2 Matrix Elements

The matrix element of an operator A^\hat{A}A^ between two states ∣ϕ⟩|\phi\rangle∣ϕ⟩ and ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ is given by:

⟨ϕ∣A^∣ψ⟩\langle\phi|\hat{A}|\psi\rangle ⟨ϕ∣A^∣ψ⟩

This complex number has important physical interpretations:

  • If ∣ϕ⟩=∣ψ⟩|\phi\rangle = |\psi\rangle∣ϕ⟩=∣ψ⟩, it gives the expectation value of A^\hat{A}A^:

    ⟨ψ∣A^∣ψ⟩\langle\psi|\hat{A}|\psi\rangle ⟨ψ∣A^∣ψ⟩

  • If ∣ϕ⟩≠∣ψ⟩|\phi\rangle \ne |\psi\rangle∣ϕ⟩=∣ψ⟩, it can represent the transition amplitude between the two states under the action of A^\hat{A}A^.

3.3 Operator Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Operators often have eigenvalues and eigenvectors. An eigenvector ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ of an operator A^\hat{A}A^ satisfies the eigenvalue equation:

A^∣ψ⟩=λ∣ψ⟩\hat{A}|\psi\rangle = \lambda|\psi\rangle A^∣ψ⟩=λ∣ψ⟩

where λ\lambdaλ is the eigenvalue associated with the eigenvector ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩. Eigenvalues represent the possible measurement outcomes for an observable, and the corresponding eigenvectors represent the quantum states associated with these outcomes.

3.4 The Role of Hermitian Operators in Measurement

In quantum mechanics, observables are represented by Hermitian operators. The expectation value of an observable A^\hat{A}A^ in a quantum state ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ is given by:

⟨ψ∣A^∣ψ⟩\langle\psi|\hat{A}|\psi\rangle ⟨ψ∣A^∣ψ⟩

This expectation value corresponds to the average value of the observable in repeated measurements of a system prepared in state ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩.

The eigenvalue spectrum of an operator A^\hat{A}A^ determines the possible measurement outcomes, and the corresponding eigenstates are the quantum states in which those outcomes would be obtained with certainty.

  • A ket ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ corresponds to this column vector representation.

  • The action of a linear operator O^\hat{O}O^ on a ket is equivalent to multiplying the corresponding matrix OOO by the state vector c⃗\vec{c}c:

    b⃗=Oc⃗\vec{b} = O \vec{c} b=Oc

    This framework allows quantum states and operator actions to be treated using the familiar tools of linear algebra. I don't need to use complex integrals

The Hermitian Adjoint and Operator Transformation

The Hermitian adjoint (or dagger) of an operator matrix is defined as the complex conjugate transpose:

(OT)∗≡O†(O^T)^* \equiv O^\dagger (OT)∗≡O†

This is the key ingredient that links the transformation of bra vectors to the conjugate-transpose of the operator. If we apply an operator O^\hat{O}O^ to a ket ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ and obtain a new state ∣ϕ⟩|\phi\rangle∣ϕ⟩:

O^∣ψ⟩=∣ϕ⟩\hat{O}|\psi\rangle = |\phi\rangle O^∣ψ⟩=∣ϕ⟩

then taking the Hermitian adjoint of both sides gives:

⟨ψ∣O^†=⟨ϕ∣\langle\psi|\hat{O}^\dagger = \langle\phi| ⟨ψ∣O^†=⟨ϕ∣

Example: Schrödinger Equation in Dirac Notation

Consider the time-independent Schrödinger equation in position space:

H^ψ(x)=Eψ(x)\hat{H} \psi(x) = E \psi(x) H^ψ(x)=Eψ(x)

where H^\hat{H}H^ is the Hamiltonian operator, EEE is the energy eigenvalue, and ψ(x)\psi(x)ψ(x) is the wavefunction of the system. The Hamiltonian typically includes kinetic and potential energy terms:

H^=p^22m+V(x),with p^→−iℏddx\hat{H} = \frac{\hat{p}^2}{2m} + V(x), \quad \text{with } \hat{p} \rightarrow -i\hbar \frac{d}{dx} H^=2mp^​2​+V(x),with p^​→−iℏdxd​

Inserting this into the Schrödinger equation gives:

[−ℏ22md2dx2+V(x)]ψ(x)=Eψ(x)\left[ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2}{dx^2} + V(x) \right] \psi(x) = E \psi(x) [−2mℏ2​dx2d2​+V(x)]ψ(x)=Eψ(x)

This differential equation can be rewritten using Dirac notation as:

H^∣ψ⟩=E∣ψ⟩\hat{H} |\psi\rangle = E |\psi\rangle H^∣ψ⟩=E∣ψ⟩

where ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ is the ket corresponding to the state with wavefunction ψ(x)\psi(x)ψ(x). This is an eigenvalue equation where:

  • H^\hat{H}H^ is the operator,
  • ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ is the eigenvector (state),
  • EEE is the eigenvalue (measurable energy).

Similarly, for a generic operator O^\hat{O}O^:

O^∣ψ⟩=λ∣ψ⟩\hat{O}|\psi\rangle = \lambda |\psi\rangle O^∣ψ⟩=λ∣ψ⟩

indicates that ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ is an eigenvector of O^\hat{O}O^ with eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ.

This compact notation helps generalize quantum mechanical problems beyond specific representations like position or momentum.

Bloch Sphere Representation of a Qubit

A qubit — the basic unit of quantum information — can be described as a superposition of two basis states, typically denoted ∣0⟩|0\rangle∣0⟩ and ∣1⟩|1\rangle∣1⟩:

∣ψ⟩=cos⁡(θ2)∣0⟩+eiϕsin⁡(θ2)∣1⟩|\psi\rangle = \cos\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) |0\rangle + e^{i\phi} \sin\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) |1\rangle ∣ψ⟩=cos(2θ​)∣0⟩+eiϕsin(2θ​)∣1⟩

This form ensures normalization:

∣cos⁡(θ2)∣2+∣sin⁡(θ2)∣2=1|\cos\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|^2 + |\sin\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|^2 = 1 ∣cos(2θ​)∣2+∣sin(2θ​)∣2=1

The parameters θ\thetaθ in [0,π][0, \pi][0,π] and ϕ\phiϕ in [0,2π][0, 2\pi][0,2π] define a point on the Bloch sphere, a geometrical representation of the qubit state as a point on the surface of a unit sphere in three-dimensional space.

  • ∣0⟩|0\rangle∣0⟩ corresponds to the north pole: θ=0\theta = 0θ=0
  • ∣1⟩|1\rangle∣1⟩ corresponds to the south pole: θ=π\theta = \piθ=π
  • Superpositions lie on the surface, with ϕ\phiϕ giving the azimuthal angle around the z-axis.

This visualization helps interpret pure qubit states geometrically and is widely used in quantum computing.

Commutativity of Operators with Common Eigenstates

Let A^\hat{A}A^ and B^\hat{B}B^ be two linear operators acting on a common eigenbasis |λ\lambdaλ, qi⟩q_i\rangleqi​⟩, where:

  • A^∣λ,qi⟩=λ∣λ,qi⟩\hat{A}|\lambda, q_i\rangle = \lambda |\lambda, q_i\rangleA^∣λ,qi​⟩=λ∣λ,qi​⟩
  • B^∣λ,qi⟩=qi∣λ,qi⟩\hat{B}|\lambda, q_i\rangle = q_i |\lambda, q_i\rangleB^∣λ,qi​⟩=qi​∣λ,qi​⟩

Then applying A^B^\hat{A}\hat{B}A^B^ and B^A^\hat{B}\hat{A}B^A^ to the same eigenstate gives:

A^B^∣λ,qi⟩=A^(qi∣λ,qi⟩)=qiλ∣λ,qi⟩\hat{A}\hat{B}|\lambda, q_i\rangle = \hat{A}(q_i|\lambda, q_i\rangle) = q_i \lambda |\lambda, q_i\rangle A^B^∣λ,qi​⟩=A^(qi​∣λ,qi​⟩)=qi​λ∣λ,qi​⟩

and similarly:

B^A^∣λ,qi⟩=B^(λ∣λ,qi⟩)=λqi∣λ,qi⟩\hat{B}\hat{A}|\lambda, q_i\rangle = \hat{B}(\lambda|\lambda, q_i\rangle) = \lambda q_i |\lambda, q_i\rangle B^A^∣λ,qi​⟩=B^(λ∣λ,qi​⟩)=λqi​∣λ,qi​⟩

Since qiλ=λqiq_i\lambda = \lambda q_iqi​λ=λqi​, we have:

[A^,B^]∣λ,qi⟩=(A^B^−B^A^)∣λ,qi⟩=0[\hat{A}, \hat{B}] |\lambda, q_i\rangle = (\hat{A}\hat{B} - \hat{B}\hat{A})|\lambda, q_i\rangle = 0 [A^,B^]∣λ,qi​⟩=(A^B^−B^A^)∣λ,qi​⟩=0

Therefore:

If two operators share a complete set of common eigenvectors, they commute on that basis:

[A^,B^]=0[\hat{A}, \hat{B}] = 0 [A^,B^]=0

This result is central in quantum mechanics, especially in contexts where simultaneous observables (like energy and angular momentum components) must be measured consistently.

On the contrary, if two operators commute then they have same eigenvectors
