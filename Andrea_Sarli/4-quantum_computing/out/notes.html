Qubit State Representation in Dirac Notation

A qubit is a vector in a two-dimensional complex vector space and can be represented in Dirac (bra-ket) notation as a superposition of the basis states ∣0⟩|0\rangle∣0⟩ and ∣1⟩|1\rangle∣1⟩:

∣ψ⟩=α∣0⟩+β∣1⟩|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle ∣ψ⟩=α∣0⟩+β∣1⟩

where α,β∈C\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{C}α,β∈C and ∣α∣2+∣β∣2=1|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1∣α∣2+∣β∣2=1 for normalization.

Examples

  • Basis state |0⟩:

    ∣0⟩=[10]|0\rangle = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} ∣0⟩=[10​]

  • Basis state |1⟩:

    ∣1⟩=[01]|1\rangle = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} ∣1⟩=[01​]

  • Superposition state |+⟩:

    ∣+⟩=12(∣0⟩+∣1⟩)=12[11]|+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} ∣+⟩=2​1​(∣0⟩+∣1⟩)=2​1​[11​]

  • Superposition state |−⟩:

    ∣−⟩=12(∣0⟩−∣1⟩)=12[1−1]|-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle - |1\rangle) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} ∣−⟩=2​1​(∣0⟩−∣1⟩)=2​1​[1−1​]

  • State with complex phase:

    ∣ψ⟩=12(∣0⟩+i∣1⟩)=12[1i]|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + i|1\rangle) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ i \end{bmatrix} ∣ψ⟩=2​1​(∣0⟩+i∣1⟩)=2​1​[1i​]

  • General state on Bloch Sphere:

    ∣ψ⟩=cos⁡(θ2)∣0⟩+eiϕsin⁡(θ2)∣1⟩|\psi\rangle = \cos\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|0\rangle + e^{i\phi} \sin\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|1\rangle ∣ψ⟩=cos(2θ​)∣0⟩+eiϕsin(2θ​)∣1⟩

    where θ∈[0,π]\theta \in [0, \pi]θ∈[0,π] and ϕ∈[0,2π]\phi \in [0, 2\pi]ϕ∈[0,2π].

Common Quantum Gates

In quantum computing, gates are unitary matrices that act on qubit states to perform operations. Among the most fundamental are:

  • Pauli-X Gate (NOT Gate):

    • Swaps |0⟩ and |1⟩.
    • Matrix form:

      X=[0110]X = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} X=[01​10​]

    Example: Applying X to ∣0⟩|0⟩∣0⟩:

    X∣0⟩=[0110][10]=[01]=∣1⟩X|0\rangle = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = |1\rangle X∣0⟩=[01​10​][10​]=[01​]=∣1⟩

    • Matrix form:

      Z=[100−1]Z = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} Z=[10​0−1​]

    Example: Applying Z to ∣1⟩|1⟩∣1⟩:

    Z∣1⟩=[100−1][01]=[0−1]=−∣1⟩Z|1\rangle = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = -|1\rangle Z∣1⟩=[10​0−1​][01​]=[0−1​]=−∣1⟩

    Example: Applying Z to ∣+⟩|+\rangle∣+⟩ and ∣−⟩|-\rangle∣−⟩:

    Recall:

    ∣+⟩=12[11],∣−⟩=12[1−1]|+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad |-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} ∣+⟩=2​1​[11​],∣−⟩=2​1​[1−1​]

      Z∣+⟩=[100−1]12[11]=12[1−1]=∣−⟩Z|+\rangle = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = |-\rangle Z∣+⟩=[10​0−1​]2​1​[11​]=2​1​[1−1​]=∣−⟩

Probability Calculation

After applying the Hadamard gate to ∣0⟩|0\rangle∣0⟩, the resulting state is:

∣ψ⟩=12(∣0⟩+∣1⟩)|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle) ∣ψ⟩=2​1​(∣0⟩+∣1⟩)

The probability of measuring ∣0⟩|0\rangle∣0⟩ is:

P(0)=∣⟨0∣ψ⟩∣2=∣12∣2=12P(0) = |\langle 0 | \psi \rangle|^2 = \left| \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \right|^2 = \frac{1}{2} P(0)=∣⟨0∣ψ⟩∣2=​2​1​​2=21​

The probability of measuring ∣1⟩|1\rangle∣1⟩ is:

P(1)=∣⟨1∣ψ⟩∣2=∣12∣2=12P(1) = |\langle 1 | \psi \rangle|^2 = \left| \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \right|^2 = \frac{1}{2} P(1)=∣⟨1∣ψ⟩∣2=​2​1​​2=21​

Thus, after applying the Hadamard gate to ∣0⟩|0\rangle∣0⟩, there is an equal probability (50%) of measuring either ∣0⟩|0\rangle∣0⟩ or ∣1⟩|1\rangle∣1⟩.

General Expression for Hadamard on ∣x⟩|x\rangle∣x⟩

The Hadamard gate acts on a single qubit ∣x⟩|x\rangle∣x⟩, where x∈{0,1}x \in \{0, 1\}x∈{0,1}, as follows:

H∣x⟩=12(∣0⟩+(−1)x∣1⟩)H|x\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \left( |0\rangle + (-1)^x |1\rangle \right) H∣x⟩=2​1​(∣0⟩+(−1)x∣1⟩)

This compact expression shows how the Hadamard gate transforms the computational basis states into the superposition basis.

Product States vs. Generic States

In quantum mechanics, especially when working with multiple qubits, it is essential to distinguish between product states and generic (entangled or non-product) states.

Product States

A product state is a multi-qubit state that can be written as the tensor product of individual qubit states. For two qubits:

∣ψ⟩=∣ψ1⟩⊗∣ψ2⟩|\psi\rangle = |\psi_1\rangle \otimes |\psi_2\rangle ∣ψ⟩=∣ψ1​⟩⊗∣ψ2​⟩

where:

∣ψ1⟩=α∣0⟩+β∣1⟩∣ψ2⟩=γ∣0⟩+δ∣1⟩|\psi_1\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle \\ |\psi_2\rangle = \gamma|0\rangle + \delta|1\rangle ∣ψ1​⟩=α∣0⟩+β∣1⟩∣ψ2​⟩=γ∣0⟩+δ∣1⟩

Then:

∣ψ⟩=(α∣0⟩+β∣1⟩)⊗(γ∣0⟩+δ∣1⟩)=αγ∣00⟩+αδ∣01⟩+βγ∣10⟩+βδ∣11⟩|\psi\rangle = (\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle) \otimes (\gamma|0\rangle + \delta|1\rangle) = \alpha\gamma|00\rangle + \alpha\delta|01\rangle + \beta\gamma|10\rangle + \beta\delta|11\rangle ∣ψ⟩=(α∣0⟩+β∣1⟩)⊗(γ∣0⟩+δ∣1⟩)=αγ∣00⟩+αδ∣01⟩+βγ∣10⟩+βδ∣11⟩

This form shows that the overall state is fully determined by the states of the individual qubits.

Generic States

A generic 2-qubit state takes the form:

∣ψ⟩=a00∣00⟩+a01∣01⟩+a10∣10⟩+a11∣11⟩|\psi\rangle = a_{00}|00\rangle + a_{01}|01\rangle + a_{10}|10\rangle + a_{11}|11\rangle ∣ψ⟩=a00​∣00⟩+a01​∣01⟩+a10​∣10⟩+a11​∣11⟩

where the coefficients aij∈Ca_{ij} \in \mathbb{C}aij​∈C are arbitrary (subject to normalization).

If this state cannot be written as a product of two single-qubit states, then it is entangled.

Example of Entangled State

The Bell state:

∣Φ+⟩=12(∣00⟩+∣11⟩)|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle) ∣Φ+⟩=2​1​(∣00⟩+∣11⟩)

Summary

  • Product state: qubits are independent.
  • Generic/entangled state: qubits are correlated in a quantum way and cannot be separated into individual components.

Controlled Gates and the CNOT

In multi-qubit quantum circuits, a very important class of operations are controlled gates. These gates apply a quantum operation (like X, Z, or H) to a target qubit only when a control qubit is in the state ∣1⟩|1\rangle∣1⟩

where x∈{0,1}x \in \{0,1\}x∈{0,1}. This notation indicates that if the control qubit is ∣0⟩|0\rangle∣0⟩, nothing happens, and if it's ∣1⟩|1\rangle∣1⟩, the unitary UUU is applied to the target.

Creating Entanglement with H + CNOT

Bell States

The Bell states (also known as EPR pairs) are a set of four maximally entangled 2-qubit states that form an orthonormal basis of the 2-qubit Hilbert space. They are:

∣β00⟩=12(∣00⟩+∣11⟩)∣β01⟩=12(∣01⟩+∣10⟩)∣β10⟩=12(∣00⟩−∣11⟩)∣β11⟩=12(∣01⟩−∣10⟩)\begin{aligned} |\beta_{00}\rangle &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|00\rangle + |11\rangle) \\ |\beta_{01}\rangle &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|01\rangle + |10\rangle) \\ |\beta_{10}\rangle &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|00\rangle - |11\rangle) \\ |\beta_{11}\rangle &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|01\rangle - |10\rangle) \end{aligned} ∣β00​⟩∣β01​⟩∣β10​⟩∣β11​⟩​=2​1​(∣00⟩+∣11⟩)=2​1​(∣01⟩+∣10⟩)=2​1​(∣00⟩−∣11⟩)=2​1​(∣01⟩−∣10⟩)​

These states are important because:

  • They are maximally entangled: measurement of one qubit instantaneously determines the state of the other.
  • They form an orthonormal basis for the 2-qubit state space.
  • They are used in protocols such as superdense coding, quantum teleportation, and entanglement swapping.

A common method to create an entangled state is by applying a Hadamard gate followed by a CNOT gate on two qubits.

Superdense Coding

Superdense coding is a quantum communication protocol that allows the transmission of two classical bits using only one qubit, provided that the communicating parties share an entangled pair in advance. This method leverages quantum entanglement and specific unitary operations to encode and decode classical information efficiently.

Quantum Teleportation

Quantum teleportation is a protocol that allows the transfer of a quantum state from one qubit to another distant qubit, without physically sending the qubit itself. It relies on entanglement and classical communication to accomplish this.

How the Protocol Works

  1. Entangled Pair:
  • Alice and Bob share an entangled pair of qubits prepared in the Bell state:

    ∣Φ+⟩=12(∣00⟩+∣11⟩)|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|00\rangle + |11\rangle) ∣Φ+⟩=2​1​(∣00⟩+∣11⟩)

  1. State Preparation:

    • Alice has a third qubit in an unknown state (∣ψ⟩=α∣0⟩+β∣1⟩(|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle(∣ψ⟩=α∣0⟩+β∣1⟩ that she wants to teleport to Bob.

  2. Bell Measurement:

    • Alice performs a Bell basis measurement on her two qubits: the one in state ψ⟩\psi\rangleψ⟩ and her half of the entangled pair.

  3. Classical Communication:

    • Alice sends the result of her measurement (2 classical bits) to Bob.

  4. Recovery:

    • Based on the received bits, Bob applies a corresponding gate (I, X, Z, or XZ) to his qubit to recover the original state ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩

This protocol does not violate causality or faster-than-light communication because it requires classical communication, but it demonstrates the power of quantum entanglement and measurement in transferring quantum information.

No-Cloning Theorem

The no-cloning theorem is a fundamental result in quantum information theory. It states that it is impossible to create an identical copy of an arbitrary unknown quantum state.

Statement

There is no unitary operation (or more generally, no quantum process) that can take an unknown state ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ and a blank state ∣0⟩|0\rangle∣0⟩ and produce two copies of ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩:

U(∣ψ⟩⊗∣0⟩)≠∣ψ⟩⊗∣ψ⟩U(|\psi\rangle \otimes |0\rangle) \neq |\psi\rangle \otimes |\psi\rangle U(∣ψ⟩⊗∣0⟩)=∣ψ⟩⊗∣ψ⟩

for all possible ∣ψ⟩|\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩.

Consequences

  • No perfect copying of quantum information.
  • Security: This underpins the security of quantum cryptography protocols (e.g., BB84), because an eavesdropper cannot clone qubits to measure them without disturbing the original state.
  • Quantum error correction must use entanglement and redundancy in more subtle ways than classical repetition codes.

This theorem highlights a key difference between classical and quantum information and is central to the design of quantum communication systems.
